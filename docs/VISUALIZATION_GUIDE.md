### What It Shows
The ground track visualization displays the **subsatellite point** - the location on Earth's surface directly beneath the satellite as it orbits. This trace shows where the satellite "shadows" on the ground over time.

### Key Elements

#### 1. **Blue Curved Line - Satellite Path**
- **What:** The satellite's trajectory projected onto Earth's surface
- **Direction:** Shows the satellite's orbital motion over time
- **Shape:** For LEO satellites, appears as a sinusoidal wave due to:
  - Earth's rotation beneath the orbit
  - Orbital inclination angle
  - Satellite velocity (~7-8 km/s for LEO)

- **Meaning:** These are moments when the satellite is visible from ground station
- **Count:** More dots = longer pass duration
- **Clusters:** Multiple clusters = multiple passes during prediction period

#### 4. **Geographic Features**
- **Coastlines:** Black outlines showing continents
- **Grid Lines:** Latitude (horizontal) and longitude (vertical) for orientation
- **Axes Labels:**

## ğŸ”¬ Understanding the Physics

### Why Passes Look This Way

#### 1. **Sinusoidal Ground Track**
- **Cause:** Combination of:
  - Satellite orbital motion (circular/elliptical path)
  - Earth rotation (15Â°/hour eastward)
  - Orbital inclination (angle to equator)
- **LEO example:** AO-91 has ~98Â° inclination (nearly polar)
  - Passes over both poles each orbit
  - Ground track crosses equator at different longitudes

#### 2. **Multiple Passes Per Day**
- **Orbital period:** ~90-100 minutes for LEO
- **Earth rotation:** ~24 hours
- **Result:** Satellite "laps" Earth ~14-16 times/day
- **Visibility:** Only ~2-5 passes are high enough elevation

#### 3. **Pass Duration Variability**
- **Short passes (2-4 min):** Low elevation, satellite near horizon
- **Long passes (5-8 min):** High elevation, satellite crosses overhead
- **Geometry:** Depends on satellite's closest approach angle

#### 4. **Elevation Peak Shape**
- **Symmetric peak:** Satellite crosses perpendicular to ground station
- **Asymmetric peak:** Satellite crosses at an angle
- **Multiple peaks:** Rare; satellite in eccentric orbit (varies altitude)
